In the heart of every town, city or rural landscape, one can find hidden realms of forgotten structures that stand as silent witnesses to the passage of time.

These desolate places, once bustling with life and purpose, now echo with emptiness, leaving behind a trail of unanswered questions and captivating mysteries

The phenomenon of abandonment can be attributed to a myriad of factors, each as unique as the buildings themselves. As we delve into this intriguing topic, we will uncover the stories of economic downturns that left once-thriving businesses desolate, natural disasters that forced communities to relocate, or historical shifts that rendered certain structures obsolete. Each abandoned building holds within its walls a frozen moment in time.

Central in the city of Halden, the house of Oscar’s street 1 has been a subject of decay. The house has been standing without any maintenance since it was damaged in two fires in 2017. (Some words from the neighbours here if I can find some interview objects).

A little further east in Halden, in Tistedal, another once great building is standing. In Torpedalsveien 17, a big house is standing with clear signs of decay. The big windows are full of old boxes and graffiti, while weed has filled the sidewalk.

In Fredrikstad, there is also abandoned buildings to find. Deindustrialisation and political reforms have made a significant impact on the county’s capital during the last 40 years.

Once one of the region’s biggest employers, Fredrikstad mekaniske verksted was a shipyard building ships and other vessels. In the late 80s, the shipyard was closed down and the remaining buildings and land was sold off to two different owners. On approximately half of the land, time has been standing more or less still the last 40 years. The old cranes and the broken windows show how the decay has been predominant.

Just across the river ‘’Vesterelva’’ is what once was an old tank facility. The main building is very visible from the road and neighbourhood. Even though the area is closed off with fences and gates, it is not hard to see that kids and other unauthorized people has been using the place as a playground. Even though it looks a lot better now, after the owners were ordered to clean up the place by the municipality.

Along the railway through Østfold there is still a few relics from the past. Lisleby station is one of them. Even though no train has stopped here since 1999, the old station building from 1917 is still standing. Challenges with vandalism has been repetitive and the neighbours have been complaining. The Norwegian railway authority has therefore put up a fence to avoid people going in.

In the heart of Fredrikstad lies what once was Østfold central hospital. Since 2015, major parts the hospital have stood and decayed. Posters of the new plans are precented, but the renovation is going slow.

In a neighbourhood in Sarpsborg, a tarp covered building is standing all by itself. By appearance, it has been standing for a long while.

Further west, in Moss, there is an industrial building left to itself. Not because of deindustrialisation but because of expansion. The old part of the insulation factory in Moss became too small and was therefore changed out with a larger facility.

Close by is an old industrial building, beautifully situated by the Oslo fjord. By the appearance of it, it is now being used as an air soft facility.

While some buildings have been subject to repurposing, others stand empty and are falling into disrepair. While visiting many of these abandoned buildings, one can often see the remains of vandalism. Most likely, this is because of children playing around. However, these ‘’playgrounds’’ are quite dangerous. Sharp objects, high altitudes and rotting construction can lead to great injuries. Therefore, the need for either renovating or demolishing is high. The question is just, who is to pay the costs? Whoever it is, the buildings stand as a silent witness to the passage of time.
